The Three Fundamental Principles of ICRP-103

1
Justification
"Any decision that alters the radiation exposure situation should do more good than harm."

Implementation:

  • Benefit-Risk Analysis: Expected benefits must outweigh radiation detriment
  • Net Positive Outcome: Overall benefit to society or exposed individuals
  • Alternative Consideration: Evaluate non-radiation alternatives
Medical Exposures

CT scan justified if diagnostic benefit outweighs cancer risk. Consider clinical need, patient age, alternative imaging.

Nuclear Power

Energy benefits vs radiation risks, environmental impact, and economic factors.

Security Screening

Airport scanners: security benefits vs minimal radiation exposure to public.

2
Optimization (ALARA/ALARP)
"The likelihood of incurring exposures, the number of people exposed, and the magnitude of their individual doses should all be kept as low as reasonably achievable (ALARA), taking into account economic and societal factors."

Optimization Process:

1
Set Dose Constraints: Upper bounds for optimization (below dose limits)
2
Identify Options: Different protective measures and their costs
3
Cost-Benefit Analysis: Evaluate cost per unit dose saved
4
Select Optimal Level: Balance protection with practicality
Exposure Category Typical Dose Constraint Dose Limit
Public (planned exposures) 0.1 - 0.3 mSv/year 1 mSv/year
Workers (routine work) 5 - 10 mSv/year 20 mSv/year
Emergency responders 100 mSv (non-lifesaving) No strict limit
3
Individual Dose Limits
"The total dose to any individual from regulated sources in planned exposure situations other than medical exposure of patients should not exceed the appropriate limits."

Purpose of Dose Limits:

  • Prevent Deterministic Effects: Keep doses below thresholds
  • Limit Stochastic Risk: Restrict probability of cancer/hereditary effects
  • Provide Regulatory Framework: Clear compliance criteria
  • Not Applicable to: Medical exposures of patients, emergency exposures

üéØ Integration of the Three Principles

Sequential Application:

  1. First - Justification: Is the practice worthwhile?
  2. Second - Optimization: How can we minimize doses while maintaining benefits?
  3. Third - Dose Limits: Have we stayed within acceptable risk levels?

Practical Example - Nuclear Medicine:

  • Justification: Diagnostic information justifies radiation risk
  • Optimization: Minimize activity, optimize imaging protocols
  • Dose Limits: Staff doses below 20 mSv/year
